First section

In Dark Patterns: Deception vs. Honesty in UI Design, Harry Brignull discusses what he feels like is a taboo topic: deception in web design. He mentions that it shouldn’t be a surprise that deception is part of web design because deception is “deeply entwined with life on this planet.” He goes on to discuss the work that is happening on darkpatterns.org to identify dark patterns and the companies that use these tactics online. The article discusses how most of the web design techniques that web designers are taught can be easily flipped to be used in this manipulative manner. A few examples he mentions as dark patterns are hidden costs, hiding negative feedback, hiding key information, free trials that roll into paid plans, etc. To conclude his article, he mentions that Expedia previously used dark patterns on their website before correcting their actions and creating a more ethical site.

In How E-Commerce Sites Manipulate You Into Buying Things You May Not Want, Jennifer Valentino-DeVries similarly discusses the use of dark patterns in commercial web design. Her primary example was a site called ThredUp which used fake names and locations to show users notifications such as “Alexandria from Anaheim” just saved $200, when in fact “Alexandria from Anaheim” did not even exist. She mentions that “the website’s code pulled combinations from a preprogrammed list of names, locations and items and presented them as actual recent purchases.” I found this example of deception to be the most ridiculous of any of the examples. While the other tactics definitely affect me more, this example was absurd. Valentino-DeVries also discusses how some senators have tried to make strides towards regulating dark patterns, but it is hard to pin down exactly what qualifies under the umbrella of dark patterns.
